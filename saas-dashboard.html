Overview

Building a day-driver for Service Analysts

This is a portfolio project similar to professional work I have done that is protected by NDA. The fifty-thousand-foot overview: build a dashboard that highlights information service analysts can take direct action on. The dashboard should serve as the primary driver of their day-to-day workflow.

I created this utilizing the Flowbite design system in place of the original proprietary design system.

"The dashboard should serve as a one-stop shop for a Service Analyst to keep tabs and prioritize work for their day."

Goal

One place to prioritize the day

The goal of this project was to create an action-driven dashboard for users responsible for scheduling and communicating repair services on various pieces of equipment. Service Analysts needed a single place to see what required attention and act on it — without flipping between the application and their email inbox constantly.

Personas

Understanding the Service Analyst

The primary persona is a Service Analyst — responsible for coordinating all service events across all equipment types.

Service Analyst
Two analysts manage all equipment in the EquiPro application Work driven by rolling compliance requirements Service events are critical to keeping equipment within compliance standards Heavy reliance on email to communicate with service facilities Usually very busy — compliance is a continuous requirement

Pain Points

  • Long, tangled email chains that are hard to track
  • Service facilities often provide partial info — requiring a brand new chain for remaining equipment
  • No easy way to find communication history for a specific piece of equipment
  • Current dashboard encompasses all equipment — can't effectively drive focus for the day
  • No ability to communicate directly from the application
  • No quick view of last communication date for a service event
Wireframes

From rough idea to testable layout

Using Figma, I created low-fidelity wireframes to show end users my rough vision for the dashboard. These wireframes focus on one widget within the overall dashboard, but the workflow established here applied to all remaining widgets.

The main layout consists of three key metrics across the top — answering: What needs to be serviced? What events need to be submitted? What equipment is headed to the service facility?

Dashboard layout wireframe — three metrics across the top

To address the email chain problem, I presented users with the idea of seeing the last communication date directly within a widget — alongside a brief preview of that communication on hover.

Widget interaction showing last communication date

Building further on getting users out of their inbox, I introduced the concept of selecting one or more events and sending communications directly from the application — reducing the constant flip between the app and email.

In-app messaging concept wireframe

UI Design

High-fidelity, then iterate

The real version of this project used an original design system built from Material components. For this portfolio version I used the Flowbite Design System to create the high-fidelity frames.

High-fidelity mockups were shown to users for feedback as part of an iterative design process — each round refining the widget drawers and actions within them.

High-fidelity dashboard frames

Takeaways

The "we" process over the "me" process

This case study covers just one widget experience out of the whole dashboard. When designing the real product, we had a series of meetings with end users — reviewing essentially the same widget over and over until we had refined it to a point of "perfection." Widget drawers and actions within them were redesigned at least half a dozen times — and that's exactly what I look for.

Ideas often sound and look great on paper. But once users see the design and prototype how their workflow will be impacted, it becomes clear where the improvements need to be made. That's my favorite part of the UX process — testing and refining high-fidelity designs with users, feeding off their excitement for the product we're designing together.

My main takeaway: understanding the importance of making the user feel like a part of the design process. The UX design process has to be a "we" process, not a "me" process.
