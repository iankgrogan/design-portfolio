Overview

Bringing Pendo to Cox Fleet — from the ground up

Pendo is a product analytics and in-app guidance platform I have integrated at two companies — KBX and Cox Automotive. At Cox Auto, other areas of the business had already been using Pendo, but I am the person spearheading its adoption specifically within the Cox Fleet apps. This is a greenfield effort: establishing the foundation, the process, and the best practices from scratch for our team.

Cox Fleet runs on Salesforce Lightning Web Apps — environments that are powerful but notoriously difficult to navigate without experience. My goal with Pendo is to reduce that friction, surface the right information at the right time, and build a continuous feedback loop between user behavior and design decisions.

Objectives

Four things Pendo helps us do better

Surface rough UX

Identify areas where users get stuck or frustrated — long click paths, confusing flows, dead ends. Analytics and session replays reveal friction that never makes it into a support ticket or feedback form.

Streamline workflows

Salesforce environments can be overwhelming for new users. Pendo guides give users clear direction up front while still allowing advanced users the freedom to work the way they want. The goal is to get users from A to B without the "Salesforce-y-ness" getting in the way.

Communicate changes

Announce new features or changes to existing ones directly within the app — where users actually are — instead of relying on emails that go unread or documentation that goes unfound. No more "wait, when did that change?" moments.

In-app release notes

Establishing a living release notes catalog via the Pendo Resource Center — giving users a persistent, always-accessible log of what's changed and when, without ever leaving the application.

Session Replays

First-person perspective on real behavior

One of the most valuable things I've set up is Pendo session replays with key team members. Traditional feedback channels — surveys, interviews, Slack messages — capture what users think they do or what they remember struggling with. Replays capture what they actually do.

The most interesting patterns to catch are the instinctual workarounds: the extra clicks, the navigation detours, the copy-paste behaviors that users have normalized to the point where they no longer mention them as pain points. They've just adapted. Replays surface these invisible frustrations and turn them into concrete design improvements.

The workarounds users don't mention are often more revealing than the problems they do. Replays make the invisible visible.

In Practice

Intake flow analysis — data over assumptions

One of the most impactful examples of Pendo in action was a business complaint that the new intake flow took 3–5x longer to complete than the legacy system. Rather than take that at face value, we used Pendo to investigate it properly.

Establishing a baseline

We ran timed test runs on the legacy system to get a concrete baseline for how long agents took to complete the intake flow end-to-end. That gave us something real to compare against rather than relying on subjective perception.

Finding the truth in the data

To analyze the new system properly, I built a dedicated Pendo dashboard focused specifically on the case intake flow. This dashboard housed the paths, journeys, and funnel reports that let us see exactly how agents were moving through the intake process — where they completed it, where they slowed down, and where they dropped off entirely. From there, we used the journey data to filter down to targeted session replays and manually track completion times against the legacy baseline.

The result: the new system was approximately 20% slower than legacy — not 3–5x. And critically, the new intake flow collects significantly more information than the legacy system did. When you account for that additional data collection, the net difference in time per unit of work completed is negligible.

The complaint was real, but the data told a different story. Pendo gave us the evidence to reframe the conversation from "this is broken" to "here's what's actually happening — and here's what we can fix."

What the replays actually revealed

While the timing data was reassuring, the completion and drop-off replays surfaced genuine friction points that were worth solving. The consistent issues we identified and prioritized for the backlog included:

  • Late duplicate discovery — Agents were reaching existing cases too late in the intake flow, after already completing a significant portion of the work, resulting in duplicate intake effort.
  • Unclear starting point — The intake flow allows agents to begin with either an asset or an account, but neither option was clearly signposted as the right starting point, causing hesitation and inconsistent paths.
  • Invalid asset workarounds — When an asset was flagged as invalid, the system lacked a clear path forward, forcing agents to develop manual workarounds to continue the flow.
  • Additional friction points — Smaller but consistent moments of confusion and hesitation that, in aggregate, contributed to the perception that the system was significantly slower than it actually was.
What I've built

Direct contributions to the integration

As the driver of this effort for Cox Fleet, I've handled the full scope of the Pendo implementation — not just the design side, but the technical setup and operational foundation as well.

  • Page and feature tagging — Mapping out and tagging all key pages and features across our Lightning Web Apps so we can accurately track adoption, usage frequency, and navigation patterns at a granular level.
  • Guide creation — Designing and building in-app guides across multiple types: lightbox announcements, tooltip walkthroughs, banners, and onboarding flows — each targeting specific user segments and triggering at the right point in the workflow.
  • Resource Center & release notes — Establishing best practices for ongoing release note management via the Pendo Resource Center, creating a scalable system for communicating changes consistently over time.
  • Pendo toolkit installation — Installing and configuring the Pendo toolkit for Salesforce into two Lightning Web Apps, handling the technical integration that makes all of the above possible.

Guide types in use

  • Lightbox announcements for major feature releases and workflow changes
  • Tooltip walkthroughs on newly redesigned or introduced screens
  • Banners surfacing time-sensitive information or action reminders
  • Onboarding flows for new users entering a Cox Fleet Salesforce environment for the first time
Why it matters

Closing the loop between design and behavior

Before Pendo, our understanding of how users navigated the Fleet apps relied on anecdotal feedback, support tickets, and what surfaced in scheduled research sessions. That's a slow and incomplete picture — and it only captures what users choose to articulate.

Pendo gives the team a continuous, quantitative signal to pair with qualitative research. It doesn't replace user interviews or usability testing — it sharpens them. It tells you exactly where to look, so research time is spent understanding the why behind what the data shows.

Takeaways

Design is never done at launch

Pendo reinforced something I already believed but now have data to prove: shipping a feature is the beginning of the design process, not the end. User behavior after launch almost always surfaces something worth improving — and having that visibility makes you a more responsive, more grounded designer.

It's also made me more intentional about designing for discoverability. If analytics show users aren't finding a feature, the instinct can be to build a guide for it — but the better first question is why the feature wasn't findable in the first place. Pendo data is most valuable when it sends you back to the design file, not just into the guide builder.

The best in-app guide is one that eventually becomes unnecessary — because the design became clear enough that users no longer needed it.
