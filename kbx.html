A small sample of real work

Goal

An internal source of financial truth

The goal of this feature was to provide screens allowing users on the finance team to accurately keep a record of accruals and invoices for their leases. This feature was not intended to be the primary source of financial truth — but to serve as an internal backup that the business could reference and compare to shared external financial systems with their customers.

Persona

The Leasing Finance Specialist

The persona for this feature is a user from the finance team focused specifically on leasing charges.

Leasing Finance Specialist
Responsible for ensuring accuracy of monthly accruals and invoices Usually very busy — lease changes throughout the month create many small charge updates that need to be vetted and verified

Pain Points

  • Legacy system forced users to go back to the search screen every time they switched between invoices — needed the ability to jump back and forth
  • No ability to approve accruals and invoices directly in the product — required going to external software
  • No ability to perform partial approvals — legacy system lacked this entirely
  • Accruals and invoicing were in separate navigation links — users wanted them in one combined space
Wireframes

Mid-fidelity by design

I do not have examples from this portion of the work that I can share, but here are the highlights of my approach.

I created mid-fidelity wireframes rather than true low-fidelity. Based on UX patterns from previous features, the structure was clear enough that lo-fi frames would have been wasted effort. These mid-fi frames used a base layout with known inputs, buttons, and table cells alongside placeholders — giving users and the PM a visual of the design direction while spurring conversations about the remaining details.

These were not meant to be a 1:1 copy of the legacy system but a modernized redesign with new functionality. The mid-fi designs served as conversation tools to gather initial sentiment and fill in the remaining gaps.

UI Design

High-fidelity samples

These designs were created using the KBX design system, which I helped establish patterns for. High-fidelity mockups were shown to users during an iterative design process, and a full prototype of the various user paths was created for testing.

Payables Search — high-fidelity frame

One notable iteration: the "Regenerate accruals" button on the Payables Search screen was added after live-environment testing. Through all discovery and design up to that point, the need to manually generate accruals had never been mentioned — it only surfaced when users tested the product in a real scenario.

Invoice detail screen — high-fidelity frame

Feature breakdown

Payables Search

Users search for accruals and invoices to approve or reject. They select between accruals and invoices, and results populate in a table. Quick approve/reject actions are available via table row selection. Clicking any row opens the invoice in detail.

Invoice Detail

Shows the full invoice with all line items. Users can approve or reject from this screen, but approval requires all line items to have an updated status (rejected, matched, or approved). A three-dot overflow menu enables status updates and manual line item additions.

A notable design decision: despite AGGrid offering in-line editing, we intentionally kept the overflow menu with actions. Users wanted to filter, select multiple items, and click one button to update statuses — the overflow pattern served this better than in-line editing, which would have added extra clicks for the same outcome.

Takeaways

Simple is often the right solution

The most challenging part of this feature was getting into the shoes of the user and understanding their workflow from a business perspective. The actual design is nothing overly complex — and that's an important takeaway. Designs don't have to be complicated to be the right solution. A straightforward layout and flow is often the correct answer, as this feature reinforced for me.

Start simple, add complexity only when the user asks for it — just as we did by adding AGGrid in place of a simple material table after user feedback.

The other lesson: the user may know everything they need, but that doesn't mean they'll tell you everything they need. Even after thorough discovery, things slip through the cracks — which is exactly why live-environment testing is so vital to the UX process.
